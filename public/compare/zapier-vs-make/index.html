Quick Verdict#

Choosing between Zapier and Make? Here’s the short version: Zapier is the easier, faster option for simple automations – connect two apps, set a trigger, and you’re done. Make (formerly Integromat) is the more powerful and affordable choice for complex, multi-step workflows with branching logic. Zapier trades complexity for speed; Make trades simplicity for control.

What Is Zapier?#

Zapier is the automation platform that made “if this, then that” accessible to non-technical users. Founded in 2011, it pioneered the concept of connecting web apps without code and remains the most widely used automation tool with over 7,000 app integrations – more than any competitor.

The core experience is building Zaps: automated workflows that trigger when something happens in one app and perform actions in another. “When a new row is added in Google Sheets, create a task in Asana” – that kind of thing. For straightforward automations like these, Zapier is unmatched in ease of use. You can build a working Zap in under five minutes without touching documentation.

Zapier has expanded significantly beyond simple two-step Zaps. Tables and Interfaces let you build lightweight databases and forms directly in Zapier. Paths enable branching logic (if the deal value is over $10K, assign to senior sales; otherwise, route to the team queue). The platform also added Zapier AI for natural-language automation building and MCP (Model Context Protocol) support, letting AI assistants trigger Zaps directly.

Pricing starts with a free plan (100 tasks/month, single-step Zaps only). The Professional plan at $19.99/month (billed annually) includes 750 tasks and multi-step Zaps. The Team plan at $69/month adds shared workspaces, SSO, and collaboration features. Enterprise pricing is custom. Tasks scale up in price as you need more – and this is where Zapier gets expensive fast.

What Is Make?#

Make (rebranded from Integromat in 2022) is the automation platform for people who think visually and need more control. Instead of Zapier’s linear step-by-step builder, Make uses a visual canvas where you drag modules, draw connections, and build workflows that can branch, loop, and merge in ways that Zapier simply can’t match.

The visual builder is Make’s defining feature. You see your entire automation as a flowchart: data flows between modules, routers split paths based on conditions, and iterators loop through arrays. For complex workflows – say, processing a webhook, checking three different conditions, calling an API, formatting the data, and sending results to different destinations based on the outcome – Make’s canvas makes the logic visible and manageable.

Make is also significantly more affordable than Zapier at scale. The free plan includes 1,000 operations/month (10x Zapier’s free tier). The Core plan at $10.59/month gets you 10,000 operations with unlimited active scenarios, 1-minute minimum run intervals, webhooks, and API access. Pro at $18.82/month adds priority execution and custom variables. Teams at $34.12/month includes team roles, shared templates, and everything in Pro. All paid plans include AI agents and custom AI provider connections (bring your own OpenAI/Anthropic key).

The operations model is more granular than Zapier’s tasks. In Zapier, each action in a Zap counts as one task. In Make, each module that processes data counts as one operation. A 5-step automation in Zapier uses 5 tasks; the same automation in Make uses 5 operations. But Make gives you far more operations per dollar.

Features#

Zapier wins on: app library (7,000+ vs Make’s 2,000+), setup speed for simple automations, built-in Tables and Interfaces, and the sheer breadth of niche app support. If the app you need is obscure, Zapier is more likely to have it.

Make wins on: workflow complexity (branching, looping, error handling), the visual builder, data transformation capabilities, API flexibility (HTTP modules for any REST API), and granular error handling at the module level. Make also includes routers and filters on all plans, including free – Zapier restricts paths to paid plans.

Ease of Use#

Zapier is easier for beginners. The step-by-step builder guides you through trigger-action pairs with clear prompts. You can build your first automation without any prior knowledge.

Make has a steeper learning curve. The visual canvas is powerful but can be overwhelming at first. Concepts like iterators, aggregators, and routers take time to understand. Once you’re past that curve (give it a weekend), Make becomes faster for building complex workflows than Zapier. But the initial investment is real.

Who Should Use What?#

Pick Zapier if: you need simple, reliable automations between popular apps and want them running in minutes. You’re a non-technical user who values ease over power. Your use case is mostly straightforward triggers and actions (new lead comes in, send to CRM, notify Slack). You need integrations with niche or industry-specific apps.

Pick Make if: you’re building complex workflows with conditions, loops, and multiple branches. Budget matters – Make gives dramatically more value per dollar. You’re comfortable with a visual builder and willing to invest an afternoon learning it. You need advanced data transformation, API calls, or error handling.

FAQ#

Is Make really that much cheaper than Zapier?#

Yes. On entry-level paid plans, Make offers roughly 13x more operations per dollar. The gap narrows slightly at enterprise tiers, but Make remains the more affordable option at every level. The main caveat: if you need a specific app integration that only Zapier has, the price difference is irrelevant.

Can I migrate from Zapier to Make?#

There’s no one-click migration tool, but the process is manageable. You’ll need to rebuild your automations in Make’s visual builder, which often takes a few hours depending on complexity. Make has equivalents for most Zapier features. Many users report that recreating their Zaps in Make actually improves them, since Make’s builder reveals optimization opportunities.

Which is better for AI workflows?#

Both are investing heavily in AI. Zapier’s AI can build automations from natural language descriptions and integrates with AI services through its app library. Make includes AI agents on all paid plans and lets you connect your own AI provider keys (OpenAI, Anthropic) on any paid plan. For custom AI pipelines, Make’s visual builder is more flexible.

Do I need to be a developer to use Make?#

No, but it helps to think systematically. Make’s visual canvas is a no-code tool, but building complex workflows requires understanding concepts like data types, conditional logic, and API basics. If you’ve ever built a complex spreadsheet formula, you can learn Make.

Our Recommendation#

For quick, simple automations, Zapier is still the fastest path from idea to working automation. The Professional plan at $19.99/month is reasonable for light usage, and the app library is unmatched.

For anything beyond basic trigger-action pairs, Make is the better choice. The Core plan at $10.59/month with 10,000 operations is exceptional value, the visual builder handles complexity that Zapier struggles with, and the per-dollar efficiency means your automation budget goes much further. If you’re willing to spend a weekend learning the interface, Make will serve you better long-term.
