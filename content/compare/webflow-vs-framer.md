**Webflow** is for teams building content-heavy, scalable websites. Its CMS handles 10,000+ items, it has native e-commerce, and the CSS-level control means you can build anything a custom-coded site can. The tradeoff is a real learning curve.

**Framer** is for startups and designers who want a stunning site live this week. The Figma-like editor, built-in animations, and simple pricing get you from idea to published faster than anything else. The tradeoff is a maturing CMS and no native e-commerce.

## Pick Webflow if...

Your site will have more than 30 pages of content. You need a blog, resource library, or directory with dynamic filtering. You need native e-commerce. SEO is a growth channel and you want schema markup, sitemaps, and redirect control. You want code export as an escape hatch.

## Pick Framer if...

You're launching a marketing site or portfolio and want it live fast. You're a designer who thinks in Figma, not CSS classes. You want smooth animations without configuring interaction panels. Your site is mostly a landing page, not a content operation. Budget is tight.

## The details

**Pricing:** Framer is cheaper for simple sites ($10 vs $14). For CMS-driven sites, they're close (Webflow $23/mo vs Framer $30/mo). At scale, Webflow's Business plan starts at $39/mo while Framer's Scale is $100/mo. Webflow's add-on model (localization, bandwidth) can push costs up in ways that aren't obvious at signup.

**Design speed:** Framer wins. The canvas feels like Figma. No CSS classes to manage, no cascading style conflicts. Designers go from mockup to live site without translating their design into a different system. Webflow's class-based styling mirrors real CSS, which gives you more power but takes weeks to learn.

**CMS:** Webflow wins by a lot. Custom content structures, 10,000+ items, dynamic pages with filtering and conditional visibility. Framer's CMS works for a startup blog with 50-100 posts. For a content marketing operation with thousands of articles, Webflow is the only option.

**E-commerce:** Webflow has it. Framer doesn't. If you need to sell products natively, the choice is made for you.

**Animations:** Both are strong but different. Framer's scroll effects and page transitions are easier to set up and look polished by default. Webflow's interactions panel is more powerful but takes longer to configure.

## FAQ

**Can Framer handle a blog?** Yes, for small blogs (50-100 posts). For content-heavy sites with complex categories and SEO architecture, Webflow's CMS is in a different league.

**Can I migrate between them?** No automated tool exists. You'd rebuild from scratch. If you think you'll outgrow Framer's CMS or need e-commerce later, start with Webflow.

**Do I need to know CSS for Webflow?** You don't write CSS, but you need to understand CSS concepts (box model, flexbox, responsive breakpoints). If those terms are foreign, expect a steeper ramp-up.
