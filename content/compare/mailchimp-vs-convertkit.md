## Quick Verdict

Choosing between **Mailchimp** and **ConvertKit** (now rebranded to **Kit**)? Here's the short version: **Mailchimp** is the all-in-one marketing platform built for e-commerce businesses and SMEs who want email campaigns, landing pages, and audience analytics in one place. **Kit** is the email platform built by creators for creators -- bloggers, podcasters, YouTubers, and course sellers who want dead-simple automations, subscriber tagging, and built-in monetization. Mailchimp gives you more marketing tools; Kit gives you a tighter creator-to-audience relationship.

## What Is Mailchimp?

Mailchimp started as a simple email newsletter tool in 2001 and has grown into one of the most recognized marketing platforms in the world. Acquired by Intuit in 2021, it now serves as a full marketing suite -- email campaigns, landing pages, social media posting, audience segmentation, and basic CRM -- all under one roof.

The core strength is its **drag-and-drop email editor** and massive template library. You pick a template, swap in your content, set your audience segment, and hit send. For e-commerce businesses specifically, Mailchimp shines with deep integrations into Shopify, WooCommerce, and other platforms. You can trigger abandoned cart emails, send product recommendations based on purchase history, and track revenue attributed to each campaign.

Mailchimp's **Customer Journey Builder** is genuinely impressive for automation. You can create multi-step, branching workflows based on purchase behavior, email engagement, site activity, and more. The visual builder makes complex sequences manageable, though the most powerful automation features are locked behind the Standard plan ($20/mo) and above.

The downside: Mailchimp has gotten expensive. The free plan was cut to just 250 contacts and 500 emails per month as of January 2026, down from 500 contacts previously. And here is the pricing gotcha that catches many users off guard -- Mailchimp counts **all** contacts toward your plan limit, including unsubscribed contacts still sitting in your account. You have to manually archive unsubscribes to stop paying for them.

## What Is Kit (ConvertKit)?

Kit (rebranded from ConvertKit in 2024) is the email platform that creators swear by. Founded by Nathan Barry in 2013, it was built specifically for bloggers, podcasters, YouTubers, and online course creators who need to grow and monetize an email list without wrestling with complex marketing software.

Kit's philosophy is **simplicity over flash**. The email editor is deliberately plain-text-focused. There are no flashy templates with columns and images everywhere -- Kit believes that simple, text-based emails feel more personal and get higher engagement. This is a feature, not a limitation, and the deliverability data backs it up. Creator emails that look like they came from a friend consistently outperform polished marketing blasts.

Where Kit really differentiates is **subscriber management and tagging**. Every subscriber gets tagged based on their behavior -- what they clicked, what they signed up for, what they bought. These tags power Kit's visual automation builder, which lets you create sophisticated sequences without complexity. The platform includes 28 pre-built automation templates covering welcome sequences, product launches, webinar funnels, and more.

Kit also has built-in **creator monetization** that Mailchimp simply does not offer. You can sell digital products, run paid newsletter subscriptions, accept tips from supporters, and tap into the Kit Creator Network for cross-promotion and sponsorship opportunities. For creators who want their email platform to also be their storefront, Kit is the clear choice.

Pricing starts with a remarkably generous **free plan** -- up to 10,000 subscribers with unlimited emails, landing pages, and forms. The catch is you only get a single automation on free. The **Creator plan** at $39/month unlocks unlimited automations and sequences. **Creator Pro** at $79/month adds subscriber scoring, advanced reporting, and Facebook custom audience integration. Annual billing saves roughly 16%.

## Head-to-Head Comparison

### Pricing

Pricing is where this comparison gets interesting, because the "cheaper" option depends entirely on your subscriber count and what you need.

| Tier | **Mailchimp** | **Kit** |
|---|---|---|
| Free plan | 250 contacts, 500 emails/mo | 10,000 subscribers, unlimited emails |
| Entry paid | $13/mo Essentials (500 contacts) | $39/mo Creator (1,000 subscribers) |
| At 1,000 contacts | ~$27/mo Essentials | $39/mo Creator |
| At 5,000 contacts | ~$75/mo Essentials | $89/mo Creator |
| At 10,000 contacts | ~$100/mo Standard | $139/mo Creator |

Mailchimp is cheaper per-contact on paid plans, especially at higher volumes. But Kit's free plan is wildly more generous -- 10,000 subscribers vs. 250. If you are a creator starting out and growing your list, Kit's free tier can carry you for a long time before you need to pay anything.

The hidden cost with Mailchimp is contact counting. Unsubscribed and non-subscribed contacts count toward your limit unless you manually archive them. Many users report their Mailchimp bill creeping up because of contacts they assumed were removed.

### Features

**Mailchimp wins on:** template variety (100+ polished designs), e-commerce integrations (abandoned cart, product recommendations, revenue tracking), built-in CRM, social media posting, landing page builder, and overall breadth of marketing tools. If you need an all-in-one marketing platform, Mailchimp covers more ground.

**Kit wins on:** subscriber tagging and segmentation, creator-focused monetization (paid newsletters, digital product sales, tip jars), the Creator Network for cross-promotion, clean automation templates, and email deliverability for text-based creator emails. Kit also offers a free WordPress plugin and integrates well with creator tools like Teachable, Gumroad, and Patreon.

### Ease of Use

Mailchimp's editor is beginner-friendly with its drag-and-drop interface, but the platform has grown complex over the years. Finding specific settings can feel like navigating a maze, and the Customer Journey Builder has a real learning curve for non-technical users.

Kit is simpler by design. The interface is clean, the automation builder is visual and intuitive, and there is less to learn because there is less bloat. Creators consistently report getting their first automation running in under 30 minutes. The tradeoff is fewer bells and whistles -- if you want a full marketing suite, Kit will feel limited.

## Who Should Use What?

**Pick Mailchimp if:** you run an e-commerce store and need deep Shopify/WooCommerce integration with purchase-based automations. You want polished, design-heavy email templates. You need an all-in-one marketing platform with landing pages, social posting, and basic CRM. Your team needs collaborative features and you are willing to pay more for breadth.

**Pick Kit if:** you are a creator -- blogger, podcaster, YouTuber, course seller -- building a direct relationship with your audience. You want to monetize your email list with paid subscriptions or digital products. You prefer simple, text-focused emails that feel personal. You are starting out and want a generous free plan to grow into. You value clean subscriber tagging over flashy templates.

## FAQ

### Is Mailchimp better than Kit for beginners?

It depends on what kind of beginner you are. If you have never sent a marketing email before and want visual templates to guide you, Mailchimp is easier to start with. If you are a creator who just wants to collect emails and send updates, Kit is simpler because there is less to learn and the free plan is far more generous.

### Can I switch from Mailchimp to Kit?

Yes. Kit has a built-in migration tool that imports subscribers, tags, and basic automation logic from Mailchimp. The process typically takes under an hour for lists under 10,000 subscribers. You will need to rebuild complex automation sequences manually, but Kit's templates make this faster than starting from scratch.

### Which has better email deliverability?

Both platforms have strong deliverability, but they approach it differently. Mailchimp's deliverability can vary because it serves a massive, diverse user base -- some senders drag down shared IP reputation. Kit's user base is smaller and more curated (mostly creators), which tends to result in slightly better deliverability rates. Kit's text-focused emails also avoid spam filters more effectively than image-heavy marketing templates.

### What about Mailchimp's free plan reduction?

As of January 2026, Mailchimp's free plan dropped to 250 contacts and 500 emails per month. Support is only available for the first 30 days, and Mailchimp branding appears on every email. For free-tier users, Kit's 10,000-subscriber free plan is dramatically more useful.

## Our Recommendation

For **e-commerce businesses** and companies that need a full marketing stack, **Mailchimp** remains the stronger choice. The e-commerce integrations, template library, and Customer Journey Builder justify the higher price if you are selling physical or digital products through a store.

For **creators and solopreneurs** building an audience, **Kit** is the better platform. The generous free plan, built-in monetization, clean tagging system, and creator-first design philosophy make it the obvious choice for bloggers, newsletter writers, podcasters, and course creators. Start on the free plan, grow to 10,000 subscribers, and upgrade to Creator when you need advanced automations.
